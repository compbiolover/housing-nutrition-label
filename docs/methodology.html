Scoring Methodology

Every dimension produces a 0–100 score using transparent, reproducible methods grounded in published data and peer-reviewed research. This page documents the approach for each dimension.

1. Disaster Resilience

The resilience score uses an Expected Annual Loss (EAL) framework — the standard approach used by FEMA's National Risk Index, Hazus, and the insurance catastrophe modeling industry.

Four Hazard Pillars

Disaster Resilience covers four perils — flood, tornado, earthquake, and fire. (It does not measure general wear-out or longevity; that is the separate Durability dimension.)

Flood: FEMA NFHL zone codes map to annual exceedance probabilities. Zone AE = 1% annual chance × 28% damage ratio = 0.28% EAL rate. Zone X (minimal) = 0.04% × 5% = 0.002% EAL rate.

Tornado: SPC historical tornado data (1950–2023) provides frequency. Point-strike probability = tornado path width ÷ 25-mile circle area. EF-based damage ratios: EF0=2%, EF1=10%, EF2=30%, EF3=60%, EF4=90%.

Seismic: USGS National Seismic Hazard Model PGA values with simplified fragility curves. Memphis sits on the New Madrid Seismic Zone with PGA 0.45–0.54g at the 2%-in-50-year level.

Fire: two contributions summed as one EAL rate. A structural/electrical base rate (≈0.02%/yr, from NFPA home-fire loss data) plus the location's wildfire EAL rate from the FEMA National Risk Index (WFIR_AFREQ × WFIR_HLRB), resolved at the census tract (county/national fallback) from a bundled national crosswalk. Both are scaled by a fire-specific modifier: wiring era (pre-1950 knob-and-tube = 1.5×, NEC 2002+ AFCI = 0.85×), construction combustibility (wood frame = 1.10×, masonry/concrete = 0.80×), and condition (same factor as the other perils). Residential sprinklers cut the peril ~60% (0.40×) in the simulator. The wildfire term makes fire genuinely location-aware — near-zero in Memphis, but the dominant peril in the fire-prone West (e.g. Los Angeles County, "Very High").

Total EAL Rate = Flood EAL + Tornado EAL + Seismic EAL + Fire EAL
(independent-hazards assumption)

Building Resilience Modifier (BRM)

Raw EAL is adjusted by a construction-quality multiplier based on four factors:

Code era (year built): Pre-1940 = 1.6× (balloon framing, no engineered connections), Pre-1970 = 1.3×, 2003+ post-IBC = 0.85×
Construction type: Wood frame = 1.2×, ICF = 0.25× PCA racking data FEMA MAT Joplin/Moore
Foundation (flood only): Full basement = 1.4×, Slab = 0.7×
Condition: Unsound = 1.5×, Excellent = 0.8×

There is no upper cap on the BRM, so old, poorly-built, and poor-condition stock can compound well above the code-current baseline (a pre-1940 unsound frame house can exceed 2.5×). Construction-type-specific floors still prevent over-crediting: wood frame floor = 0.50, ICF floor = 0.15 (85% max EAL reduction).

Above-Code Feature Modifiers

20+ toggleable features with literature-backed multipliers:

Wind: Hurricane straps (0.70), hip roof (0.55), impact garage door (0.75), sealed roof deck (0.80), metal roof (0.75) IBHS research
FORTIFIED: Roof tier (0.35), Silver (0.25), Gold (0.20) 73–76% claim reduction, Hurricane Sally actuarial data
Seismic: Cripple wall bracing (0.45), seismic retrofit (0.75) PEER-CEA research
Flood: Elevation +1ft above BFE (0.15), +2ft (0.08), +3ft (0.04) FEMA depth-damage curves

Adjusted EAL = Raw EAL × BRM × Π(bonus modifiers)

Score Mapping

Total EAL rate maps to 0–100 via log-linear interpolation: score 100 at EAL ≤ 0.001%, score 80 at 0.02%, score 60 at 0.1%, score 40 at 0.3%, score 20 at 1%, score 0 at ≥ 2%.

2. Energy Efficiency

Score based on Energy Use Intensity (EUI) in kBTU/sqft/yr, using DOE Building America and EIA RECS 2020 benchmarks for IECC Climate Zone 4A.

EUI Lookup by Building Archetype

Each parcel maps to an archetype via year built (vintage bin), square footage (size bin), construction type, and HVAC system from the Shelby County CAMA database.

Published CZ 4A EUI benchmarks by vintage: Pre-1950 = 75 kBTU/sqft/yr, 1950–1979 = 55, 1980–1999 = 40, 2000–2009 = 30, 2010+ = 25, Passive House = 12.

Construction modifiers: ICF = 0.65× (superior thermal mass), SIP = 0.70×. Multi-unit shared-wall bonus: 2 units = 0.85×, 3–4 = 0.80×, 5+ = 0.75×. Passive house certification = 0.40×. Solar = 60% electricity cost offset.

Cost Estimation

Memphis Light Gas & Water / TVA rates: $0.105/kWh electricity, $1.10/therm gas. Electric/gas split based on heating system type from CAMA data.

Score Mapping

Score 100 at EUI ≤ 15 (passive house), 80 at 25, 60 at 40, 40 at 55, 20 at 70, 0 at ≥ 90

3. Durability

A component-lifespan / effective-age model — how much usable service life the building has left, tempered by the assessor's observed condition. (Distinct from Disaster Resilience, which measures hazard loss rather than wear-out.)

Component Service-Life Basket

Eight major systems each carry a typical service life and a replacement-cost weight; the long-lived shell dominates and short-cycle systems contribute less:

Structural shell 100 yr (0.30) · plumbing 55 yr (0.10) · electrical 35 yr (0.10) · roof covering 25 yr (0.15) · windows 25 yr (0.08) · interior finishes 20 yr (0.10) · HVAC 18 yr (0.12) · water heater 12 yr (0.05).

Each system's remaining-life fraction is clamp((service_life − effective_age) / service_life, 0..1), using the assessor's effective year built (EFFYR, which folds in major renovations) rather than the original year. The age score is the weighted mean × 100.

Condition & Adjustments

base = 0.55 × condition_score + 0.45 × age_score

Condition comes from the CAMA CDU rating (Excellent = 100, Average = 60, Unsound = 0; the numeric COND 0–5 field is the fallback) and is weighted slightly above pure age because an inspector's observation captures real-world maintenance. The base is then scaled by exterior-wall material and by construction grade (linear around the dataset midpoint, clamped 0.90–1.12×).

Parcels with neither a build year nor a condition rating — vacant / non-residential land — are left unscored and excluded from the composite rather than guessed.

4. Environmental Footprint

A life-cycle CO₂e score blending three components — 0.50 operational + 0.30 embodied + 0.20 water — each normalized 0–100 against published good-vs-poor benchmarks (log-linear; higher score = lower footprint).

Operational

operational = annual_kWh × 0.423 kg CO₂e/kWh + annual_therms × 5.3 kg CO₂e/therm

The grid factor is EPA eGRID2022, subregion SRTV (SERC / Tennessee Valley); the gas factor is from the EPA GHG Emission Factors Hub. Energy use is the same model the Energy dimension produces. (The grid factor is a dated constant — refresh it on each eGRID release as the grid decarbonizes.)

Embodied & Water

Embodied: a material/size estimator keyed on exterior wall and construction grade, calibrated to the verified US single-family band of ~39–121 kg CO₂e/m², then amortized over the shell's expected service life (EN 15978 / RICS 60-year reference study period) — so more durable construction spreads its upfront carbon over more years.

Water: EPA WaterSense usage benchmarks, with the Memphis Sand aquifer's low embedded-energy advantage.

Parcels with no living area (vacant / non-residential) are left unscored.

5. Infrastructure Burden

Models the annual municipal cost to serve each parcel using a density-based cost allocation approach. Inspired by the Halifax cost-of-sprawl study, Strong Towns fiscal analysis, and Smart City Memphis's density-budget research.

Per-Service Cost Model

Costs decrease with density (dwelling units per acre):

Roads: <1 DU/ac = $2,400/yr → 12+ DU/ac = $400/yr
Water/sewer: <1 DU/ac = $1,500/yr → 12+ DU/ac = $350/yr (base) + $200/unit usage
Fire/EMS: ~$800 base × distance modifier
Police: ~$1,200 base × density modifier
Sanitation: $504/unit (Memphis's actual fee)
Parks: $300/unit

Local Calibration (per county)

These per-household figures are the Memphis pilot calibration, which sets the cost-to-serve shape (how cost falls with density). The cost level is then scaled to each county's actual local-government spending using the U.S. Census of Governments — per-capita direct expenditure by function (roads, water/sewer, fire, police, sanitation, parks), normalized to Shelby County. So a county that spends 2× the Memphis per-capita rate on roads gets 2× the road cost. Shelby is 1.0 on every function (the pilot is unchanged); Los Angeles County, for example, runs ~2.0× on roads and ~2.6× on water/sewer (2022 census). Counties not in the crosswalk fall back to the national average.

Fiscal Ratio

Fiscal Ratio = Municipal Property-Tax Revenue / Non-School Infrastructure Cost
Property Tax (Shelby) = Appraised Value × 25% assessment × $3.19/$100 (Memphis rate)

A ratio > 1.0 = net contributor to the city. The median Shelby County parcel has a ratio of 0.27 — generating only 27 cents per dollar of infrastructure cost.

Outside Shelby: both sides of the ratio are localized per county and put on a like-for-like non-school basis. The cost side is the Census of Governments municipal spending (above, schools excluded). The revenue side starts from each county's effective property-tax rate (median real-estate taxes ÷ median home value, U.S. Census ACS), then nets out the school-district share of property tax (also Census of Governments; ~41% nationally) so it matches the school-excluded cost side — and where a county funds schools through its general government (a "dependent" system with no separate district, so the school share reads ~0), the national-average share is used instead. The home value defaults to the county median when you don't enter one, so the revenue estimate reflects the local market. These remain county-level estimates (a county median, not a parcel's actual millage or value); sub-county / per-jurisdiction millage (state DOR tables) is a future precision refinement.

Score Mapping

Score 100 at ratio ≥ 0.98, 80 at 0.53, 60 at 0.36, 40 at 0.26, 20 at 0.17, 0 at ≤ 0.09

These breakpoints are anchored to the national distribution of fiscal ratios — a population-weighted reference over U.S. counties × residential-density archetypes, computed with the localized non-school cost & revenue model — so the score tracks national percentile rank (A = top ~20%, B = 60–80th, C = 40–60th, D = 20–40th, F = bottom ~20%). On the non-school basis the national median fiscal ratio is ~0.31 (the typical U.S. home covers only about a third of its non-school municipal cost from non-school property tax), so a typical home scores around 50 (C). This replaced the original Shelby-pilot-anchored thresholds once cost and revenue were localized per county.

Density on this parcel (the density dividend)

Because the cost model is density-driven, the same lot scores very differently as a single-family home, a duplex, a triplex, or a quadplex. The density comparison holds the location and lot fixed and varies the number of dwelling units, keeping the per-unit value constant (so total appraised value scales with units). As units increase, the same land and municipal services are shared across more homes, so per-unit cost-to-serve falls and the fiscal ratio — and Infrastructure Burden grade — improve. That gap is the density dividend. It's available from the density_comparison() helper, the housing-simulate --density CLI flag, the GET /density API endpoint (e.g. ?address=…&units=1,2,4), and the “What if this parcel were denser?” button under the address search on the home and examples pages.

6. Health Impact

Uses CDC PLACES census-tract-level health estimates. Nine measures: obesity, diabetes, high blood pressure, asthma, coronary heart disease, mental distress, physical inactivity, stroke, COPD.

Each parcel is assigned to its census tract via the Census Bureau geocoder. Health index = average of inverted percentile ranks across all measures (lower prevalence = higher score). Range in Shelby County: 0.7 to 95.5 — nearly the full 0–100 spectrum, reflecting extreme neighborhood health disparities.

Key finding: Obesity ranges from 28% to 58% and diabetes from 7% to 34% between the healthiest and least healthy Memphis neighborhoods — a 2× to 5× gap within the same county.

7. Socioeconomic

Census ACS 5-year estimates at the census tract level. Indicators include median household income, poverty rate, educational attainment (bachelor's degree or higher), and unemployment rate. Indexed using inverted percentile ranks (higher income, lower poverty = higher score).

8. Walkability

Uses the Walk Score API, which returns three 0–100 sub-scores for a parcel's coordinates: a Walk Score (access to daily errands on foot), a Transit Score (proximity and quality of public transit), and a Bike Score (bike infrastructure and terrain).

Composite Weighting

Walk Score is already a 0–100 score, so the raw Walk Score is used directly as the dimension score. Where a parcel also has a transit and bike score, a weighted composite is taken — deliberately weighted toward walkability, since walking access to daily needs matters most for everyday life:

Walkability = 0.60 × Walk + 0.25 × Transit + 0.15 × Bike
(parcels missing transit or bike fall back to the raw Walk Score)

The result carries the same dual grading as every other dimension — a national absolute grade and a local percentile grade within Shelby County. Because the Walk Score is already nationally calibrated (0 = car-dependent, 90–100 = a "walker's paradise"), the national grade is directly comparable across cities.

Context: Shelby County is overwhelmingly car-dependent suburban and exurban development, so most parcels score low on the national absolute scale. The local percentile grade is what surfaces the genuinely more walkable pockets — older street-grid neighborhoods near Midtown and Downtown Memphis — relative to their neighbors.

9. Climate Projections

Sub-county downscaled climate-hazard projections from the USGS CMIP6-LOCA2 threshold/extreme-event metrics — the Weighted Multi-Model Mean (an ensemble mean over CMIP6 LOCA2-downscaled models, ~6 km), sampled at each census tract's internal point (county = the mean of its tracts). Three hazard legs are blended equally into a 0–100 score (higher = less projected hazard): extreme heat (annual days > 95°F and > 100°F), heavy precipitation & flood (days > 1″ and the annual max 5-day total), and drought (max consecutive dry days). Each metric is scored against national breakpoints anchored to the SSP2-4.5 mid-century distribution. The dimension is reported as a low/high band from SSP2-4.5 → SSP5-8.5 at mid-century (2040–2069), with the SSP2-4.5 value as the headline.

Caveat: the ~6 km grid is sampled at a tract's internal point — a real sub-county value, but not parcel-resolution precision. Values are a multi-model ensemble mean; a tract resolves at tract level, falling back to its parent county then the national average where the grid has no data (e.g. outside CONUS).
Tracts vs. the CMRA tract layer: these tract values come from sampling the LOCA2 ~6 km grid, so tracts within a large, diverse county genuinely differ (San Bernardino spans coast to desert). That is the inverse of CMRA's ArcGIS tract layer, which we found broadcasts the single county value onto every tract polygon (zero sub-county signal) and so was deliberately not used.

Dual Grading Details

National absolute grade: A ≥ 80, B ≥ 60, C ≥ 40, D ≥ 20, F < 20. Calibrated for national comparison — a Grade B in Memphis means the same thing as a Grade B in Phoenix.

Local percentile grade: A = top 10%, B = next 25% (65th–90th percentile), C = next 30% (35th–65th), D = next 25% (10th–35th), F = bottom 10%. All five grades are always populated by construction.

Composite score: Simple NaN-skipping mean of all active dimension scores.
